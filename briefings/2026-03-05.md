## 🧱 BUILDER INTELLIGENCE

**Methodology beat:**

Simon Willison published a sharp anti-pattern: don't file pull requests with code you haven't reviewed yourself. If you open a PR with hundreds of lines of agent-generated code you haven't verified, you're delegating the actual work to others. For you as a non-technical CEO supervising AI-generated code: **you are the reviewer**. If you're shipping Cursor output you haven't read, this is your blind spot.

The Harness Engineering debate from Latent Space adds a useful frame: Noam Brown of OpenAI argues that before reasoning models, engineers built complex agentic scaffolds — but reasoning models have replaced the need for most of that scaffolding, and the remaining scaffolds will also be replaced as models improve. Translation for you: **don't over-engineer your prompting systems. The model is getting smarter faster than your scaffolding can.**

Cursor is now valued at $50B — a data point from the Latent Space piece confirming that the tool you use daily is now a tier-1 infrastructure company, not a startup experiment.

**Micro-SaaS Radar:**

The "give an agent a box" pattern — isolated sandboxes for agents with managed file permissions and identity — is described as "one of the single hottest areas in AI infrastructure growing 100% MoM." The insight for micro-SaaS: products that give AI agents clean, permissioned access to a vertical's files and data are still wide open. Box is doing it for enterprise. Nobody is doing it well for specialty retail, restaurant ops, or small business contexts.

Box CEO Aaron Levie argues that all enterprise files — contracts, research, memos, marketing material — that previously sat dormant are now active raw material for agents. The new value isn't storing files; it's making them queryable and actionable by agents. Micro-SaaS angle: a shop owner's operational docs, supplier contacts, and staff notes are the same problem at a smaller scale. An agent that knows your shop's files is a real product.
