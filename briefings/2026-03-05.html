🌍 WORLD LENS

China sets its lowest GDP growth target in decades — and admits it.

China has set its lowest GDP growth target in decades, warning of "challenges" ahead. The paywalled FT article prevents deeper analysis, but the signal itself is the story: Beijing is managing expectations downward in public, which it rarely does without cause. This is not a single-quarter adjustment — it reflects structural deflationary pressure, a property sector that has not cleared, and an export machine running into tariff walls from both Washington and Brussels simultaneously.

For a specialty food shop in Brussels, the transmission mechanism is indirect but real. European consumer confidence tracks global uncertainty. If China's slowdown deepens, the commodity input costs that affect Chez Julien's suppliers (packaging, logistics, processed food inputs) may ease — but the deflationary signal also means European households will feel pressure to trade down. Premium specialty food is not immune to that rotation. The more proximate risk is the EU's ongoing reconfiguration of trade dependencies away from China: supply chains for packaging, certain ingredients, and hospitality equipment are quietly getting longer and more expensive.

The broader macro frame for a Brussels founder in 2026: the global economy is entering a phase of managed deglobalisation — slower, more expensive supply chains, higher regulatory friction, and consumers who are cautious even when employed. That is a headwind for discretionary specialty retail and a neutral-to-tailwind for software (which has no supply chain).

Iran-US tensions (referenced in the March 2 briefing, The Economist) remain a slow-burn input cost risk through energy prices. Euro energy costs are the single largest macro variable affecting Belgian small business operating margins. No escalation this week, but the structural fragility persists.
