## 🚨 Iran / Strait of Hormuz: Energy Crisis Update — March 12, 2026

### 📌 What Changed

- The conflict caused immediate volatility in energy markets, with Brent crude oil prices surging 10–13% to around $80–82 per barrel by **March 2, 2026**, as Iran's closure of the Strait of Hormuz disrupted 20% of global oil supplies.

- By **March 9**, oil prices topped **$100 a barrel** for the first time since Russia's invasion of Ukraine, as the US and Israel continued to strike Iran and the Islamic Republic retaliated with missiles and drones across the Middle East.

- As of **March 11**, Iran's IRGC threatened that "not one litre of oil" would pass through the Strait of Hormuz. A maritime security expert told Al Jazeera: "For the shipping industry right now, it's **impossible** to go through the Strait of Hormuz."

- The US-Iran conflict has pushed the Strait of Hormuz to a **de facto closure through insurance withdrawal**, putting at risk roughly 20% of global oil supply alongside critical volumes of jet fuel, LPG, and LNG.

---

### ⚡ Impact on European Energy Costs

- Following coordinated strikes by Israel and the US against Iran on **February 28**, Brent crude rose above **$84/barrel** (up from ~$70), while **natural gas prices in Europe and the UK spiked even more sharply** — the impact on gas has been even more pronounced than the comparable 2025 oil spike.

- On the morning of **March 2**, European gas prices spiked about **20%**. The longer-term impact will depend on how long hostilities last and their effect on Hormuz shipping.

- Europe's most pronounced vulnerability is **LNG**. If LNG flows via Hormuz are curtailed, global spot availability tightens immediately, forcing Europe to compete with Asian buyers for cargoes — a dynamic seen during the 2021–2023 energy crisis. This is worsened by the fact that Europe started 2026 with much lower gas storage: **46 bcm** at end of February, versus **60 bcm** in 2025 and **77 bcm** in 2024.

- Apart from oil, **30% of Europe's jet fuel** supply originates from or transits via the Strait, and **one-fifth of global LNG** passes through the waterway.

---

### 📊 Why It Matters

- A sustained increase in energy costs could trigger a **surge in inflation and squeeze economic growth**, raising fears of **stagflation**.

- Goldman Sachs estimates that traders are demanding about **$14 more per barrel** as a risk premium (as of March 3). That premium roughly corresponds to their estimate of the effect of a **full four-week halt** in Hormuz flows, with spare pipeline capacity used as a partial offset.

- Around **a quarter of global seaborne oil** and **one-fifth of LNG shipments** pass through Hormuz. When energy prices rise at this scale, income is transferred from energy-importing countries (like most of Europe) to energy exporters.
